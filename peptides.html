Repair & Recovery · Andrew Huberman
BPC-157 for Injury Healing & Tissue Repair
How a gastric peptide became the most talked-about recovery compound in biohacking
Key Takeaways
  • BPC-157 (Body Protective Compound-157) is a 15-amino-acid synthetic peptide derived from a protein naturally found in human gastric juice. Its repair properties come from promoting angiogenesis — new blood vessel growth — in damaged tissue.
  • Huberman used BPC-157 after a lumbar spine injury, crediting it alongside physical therapy for accelerated recovery. The compound appears to reduce inflammation while simultaneously promoting tissue regeneration.
  • The mechanism centers on the VEGFR2-Akt-eNOS signaling axis — it upregulates vascular growth factors and stabilizes tissue barriers, which explains why effects appear across tendons, ligaments, gut, and nerve tissue.
  • BPC-157 has notable route flexibility: subcutaneous injection near target tissue for localized effects, or oral/systemic use for gut-related applications. The peptide is stable in gastric juice, unlike most.
  • Critical caveat: virtually all research is in animal models. Human clinical trials are limited. The credibility question is real — but so is the widespread anecdotal use among athletes and biohackers.

Injury Recovery · Thomas DeLauer
The BPC-157 & TB-500 Wolverine Stack
Why combining these two healing peptides is greater than the sum of their parts
Key Takeaways
  • BPC-157 and TB-500 work through entirely different mechanisms — making their combination synergistic rather than redundant. BPC-157 drives angiogenesis via VEGFR2; TB-500 works through G-actin sequestration and actin polymerization dynamics.
  • TB-500 (synthetic Thymosin Beta-4) promotes cell migration and differentiation — meaning it recruits the cells to the injury site that BPC-157 then helps establish with new vasculature. Together: faster, more complete repair.
  • The "Wolverine Stack" name reflects the regenerative effect observed — particularly notable in tendons, ligaments, and cartilage where blood supply is naturally limited and healing is slow.
  • Protocol typically: BPC-157 at 200–500mcg subcutaneous daily, TB-500 at 2–5mg twice weekly. Both are WADA-prohibited and BPC-157 is now FDA Category 2 (restricted from compounding as of 2024).
  • The combination reduces delayed onset muscle soreness through temperature mechanisms not fully understood — making it attractive for both injury recovery and performance maintenance.

Metabolic Health · Peter Attia MD
GLP-1 Agonists — The Future of Treating Obesity?
A clinician's honest assessment of Ozempic, Wegovy, and what the data actually shows
Key Takeaways
  • GLP-1 is a peptide naturally produced by your gut. GLP-1 agonist drugs (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro) are essentially exogenous versions that restore the gut-brain-pancreas signaling loop — making the body "talk to itself" again about hunger and satiety.
  • Semaglutide trials showed remarkable results for weight loss and metabolic parameters, but the muscle loss risk is the underappreciated threat. Attia's consistent message: protein intake and resistance training are non-negotiable while on GLP-1s.
  • GLP-1s reduce systemic inflammation, improve insulin sensitivity, normalize glucose, improve blood pressure and lipid profiles — suggesting true metabolic recalibration rather than a single-pathway weight loss effect.
  • The nausea side effect (lasting ~8 weeks for most patients) is the mechanism working — it blunts appetite at the neurological level. Most patients tolerate it because the results justify it.
  • 92% of physicians surveyed either use or recommend GLP-1s to patients. The clinical consensus has moved decisively — the remaining question is long-term use, cycling, and who should not take them.

A Fat Loss Peptide More Powerful Than GLP-1
The emerging compound giving GLP-1 drugs a run for their money — without the nausea
Key Takeaways
  • Not all fat loss peptides work through the same pathway as GLP-1s. Emerging compounds target brown fat activation, AMPK signaling, and mitochondrial biogenesis — offering fat loss through metabolic efficiency rather than appetite suppression alone.

Science Deep Dive · Surgeon Review
Peptide Injections — Does the Science Actually Hold Up?
A surgeon's honest look at what the research does — and doesn't — support on BPC-157 and TB-500
Key Takeaways
  • The honest answer on peptides: mechanistic plausibility is high, human clinical data is almost nonexistent. Most research is in rats. The theoretical science makes sense — the human evidence is not there yet.
  • BPC-157 promotes new blood vessel formation, which accelerates tissue repair. The same mechanism that heals could theoretically accelerate growth of precancerous cells — a real concern that promoters consistently underemphasize.
  • TB-500's actin-regulating mechanism is biologically sound and well-understood in cell biology. The translation to clinical wound healing and tissue repair in humans has early support but needs large-scale trials.
  • The gray market contamination problem: unregulated peptides have unknown purity, dosing, and sterility. The risk isn't just effectiveness — it's infection, mislabeling, and unknown interactions.
  • The surgeon's verdict: promising compounds worth watching, not worth self-experimenting with outside a clinical relationship. The gap between biohacker hype and clinical evidence remains wide.

Advanced Protocols · Dave Asprey & Jay Campbell
Advanced Peptide Stacks — Mitochondrial, Growth Hormone & GLP-1
What happens when biohacking veterans stack peptides with precision — and what they've learned from getting it wrong
Key Takeaways
  • Mitochondrial peptides (MOTS-c, Elamipretide/SS-31) represent the next frontier — targeting energy production at the cellular level rather than hormonal or structural repair. They show promise for aging, metabolic disease, and endurance.
  • Growth hormone peptides (Tesamorelin, Ipamorelin, CJC-1295) work well but MK-677 — often positioned as a convenient oral alternative — comes with appetite dysregulation and water retention tradeoffs that make it less clean for most protocols.
  • Klotho peptide: emerging data suggests it protects endothelial function and kidney health at small doses. At higher doses, kidney risk increases. Asprey and Campbell emphasize micro-dosing precision over aggressive stacking.
  • GLP-1 + muscle preservation stack: for anyone on a GLP-1 drug, adding a GH secretagogue (like Ipamorelin) and ensuring high protein intake addresses the primary failure mode — muscle wasting in a caloric deficit.
  • The meta-lesson: peptide stacking is a skill that requires understanding pharmacokinetics, receptor sensitivity, and timing. The biohacker who treats it like supplement stacking will get suboptimal results or harm.

Complete Protocol Guide
The Ultimate Guide to Peptides — BPC-157 & TB-500 Complete Protocols
Everything you need to understand before starting — dosing, cycling, stacking, and what to avoid
Key Takeaways
  • BPC-157 protocol essentials: 200–500mcg subcutaneous, once daily, injected near the target tissue for localized injury. For systemic or gut applications, oral dosing at lower amounts (around 250mcg) is viable given its gastric stability.
  • TB-500 protocol: 2–5mg subcutaneous, twice weekly in the acute phase; drop to once weekly for maintenance. Unlike BPC-157, TB-500 works better at distance from the injury site — systemic administration is preferred.
  • Cycling matters: continuous use beyond 8–12 weeks risks receptor downregulation. A 4-week off cycle restores sensitivity. Most experienced users run 8 weeks on, 4 weeks off.
  • The Wolverine Stack combined protocol: run both simultaneously for the acute phase (4–6 weeks), then drop to BPC-157 maintenance only. The synergistic phase is where the most dramatic results occur.
  • What to never do: mix peptides with oil-based compounds in the same syringe, skip reconstitution with bacteriostatic water, store improperly (refrigerate, protect from light), or source from gray market research chemical sites.
